## Core Conclusion

The AI power bottleneck is real enough to justify serious long-term research, but the broad "buy nuclear/SMR for AI" shortcut should be blocked.

The workflow routes the theme to `industry_map_first` because public-company value capture differs sharply by node:

- existing nuclear generators have the clearest monetization path, but still need contract economics, restart/capex, regulatory and valuation work
- private SMR developers have high theme purity but limited direct public-market expression
- listed advanced-nuclear developers have high purity but pre-commercial execution and valuation burden
- fuel and grid suppliers are investable read-throughs, not pure nuclear AI-power exposure
- hyperscalers are buyers of power, not direct nuclear profit-pool owners

The correct next step is not a broad actionability conclusion. It is a node map plus selected single-equity follow-ups where contract/regulatory quality and expectation maps can be completed.

## Facts

- IEA and EIA treat data centers and AI as material electricity-demand drivers that require power-system planning.
- Constellation and Microsoft announced a 20-year power purchase agreement connected to the planned restart of the former Three Mile Island Unit 1 site as the Crane Clean Energy Center.
- Google and Kairos Power announced an advanced-nuclear agreement targeting deployment by 2030 and more by 2035.
- Amazon announced nuclear-energy agreements including an X-energy relationship and Energy Northwest project pathway.
- NRC advanced-reactor materials show that licensing status is a project-specific gate.
- FERC public materials treat data-center co-location and AI-related power demand as an active regulatory and grid issue.

## Inferences

- Hyperscaler demand is likely real, but demand is not the same as public-equity value capture.
- Existing nuclear assets may have a nearer-term value-capture path than SMR developers because they can monetize scarce firm clean power through contracts or market power.
- SMR equity optionality is more exposed to licensing, project execution, financing and valuation-duration risk than to near-term demand risk.
- Co-location and cost-allocation rules can change the economics of a headline data-center nuclear contract.

## Judgments

The workflow should add a new overlay:

`power-contract-regulatory-check.csv`

Reason:

The current long-term method already covers macro, industry, execution and valuation, but this case shows that power/nuclear theses need a more explicit contract/regulatory gate. A signed demand announcement is insufficient if contract margin, offtaker terms, regulatory status, interconnection, cost allocation and commercial operation timing are unknown.

## Candidate Follow-Up Cases

1. `CEG`: best candidate for a full single-equity case because it has direct public-company value-capture evidence through the Microsoft PPA. Required gates: expectation map, restart capex, contract margin, nuclear fleet risk and regulatory milestones.
2. `VST` or `TLN`: IPP / power-market exposure cases. Required gates: merchant power expectations, data-center contract exposure, leverage and capital allocation.
3. `CCJ` or `LEU`: nuclear fuel / enrichment read-through cases. Required gates: contracting duration, fuel-cycle bottleneck, geopolitics and valuation expectations.
4. `OKLO` or `SMR`: high-purity optionality cases. Required gates: NRC status, project COD, financing, revenue bridge, unit economics and valuation expectation map.

## Stop Rules

- Do not treat hyperscaler nuclear demand as a public-company thesis unless the value-capture node is explicit.
- Do not treat a headline PPA as durable value capture unless contract economics, offtaker credit, regulatory status, interconnection and COD are evidenced.
- Do not treat SMR purity as actionability when revenue, licensing and project delivery are still source gaps.
- Do not use nuclear scarcity as a valuation conclusion without an expectation map.
